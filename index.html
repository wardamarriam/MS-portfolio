About The Course

What is Management Science?

Management Science is the use of structured, quantitative thinking — models, data, and logical frameworks — to make better decisions under real-world constraints like budgets, time, and uncertainty. Instead of guessing or relying purely on intuition, it gives decision-makers a clear way to define their goal, identify their limits, and find the choice that performs best within those limits. This semester, every project in this portfolio applied that same core idea to a different kind of problem.

The Pizza Optimizer 🍕

Hands-on spreadsheet activity · Management Science

Binary VariablesObjective FunctionConstraintsSensitivity Analysis

Project Overview

This activity introduced the core structure of a Management Science model through a fun but mathematically real problem: choosing the best pizza toppings under a 7.00ww budget. Each topping has a known cost and satisfaction score, and the goal is to select a combination that maximizes total satisfaction without exceeding the budget.

What makes this a proper management science problem is its structure. The toppings are the set, their costs and scores are parameters, whether each topping is selected is a binary decision variable (1 = yes, 0 = no), the sum of satisfaction scores is the objective function to maximize, and the budget is the constraint. I built a spreadsheet model to test combinations and then performed sensitivity analysis to understand how the optimal solution shifts when inputs change.

This matters because the same logic — maximize an outcome subject to constraints using structured variables — underlies real-world decisions in production scheduling, resource allocation, and supply chain planning.

Decision Tree Construction 🌳

Group activity · Scenario 2 — The Sleeping Dragon · Partner: Khadicha (2417758)

Decision TreeUncertaintyBranching LogicScenario Analysis

Project Overview

A decision tree is a Management Science tool that maps out every possible choice and its consequences in a structured, visual way — branching from an initial decision node through chance events and further decisions until every outcome is captured. It forces decision-makers to think ahead before acting, rather than reacting to problems as they arise.

In this activity, Khadicha and I were given Scenario 2 — The Sleeping Dragon: a kingdom trapped in endless winter, with a sleeping dragon beneath the northern mountains. The legends are split — the dragon may be causing the winter, or it may be suppressing something far more dangerous underground. The king's council must decide what to do. We built a full decision tree across five levels mapping all major choices: investigate, attack, leave, or work with the dragon — and the downstream consequences of each.

Our recommended path was to investigate first — send explorers and mages, study ancient texts, speak with northern tribes, and discover why the dragon is sleeping before committing to any irreversible action. This avoids unnecessary danger and ensures the kingdom makes an informed decision rather than acting blindly.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

A decision tree makes implicit reasoning explicit. By drawing every branch before deciding, we could see that attacking first closes off the "work with dragon" branch permanently — an irreversible move. Investigating first keeps all branches open. This taught me that in decision analysis, preserving optionality has real value.

Skills

I practised structuring a complex, ambiguous problem into decision nodes, chance events, and outcomes — the same structure used in business decisions involving uncertainty. Building the tree collaboratively also required us to agree on what counted as a branch versus a sub-outcome, which sharpened my logical thinking.

Reflection

The scenario was fictional, but the lesson was real: acting without information is almost always more costly than gathering it first. The "attack immediately" branch led to the worst outcomes in every sub-path we traced. I would apply this framework to any real decision involving uncertainty — from business strategy to personal choices.

Test Your Ability — ILP Profit Maximiser 🏭

Handwritten activity · Integer Linear Programming · Production Planning

ILPObjective FunctionConstraintsInteger Variables

Project Overview

This activity introduced Integer Linear Programming (ILP) — a type of Management Science model where decisions must be whole numbers (integers) rather than fractions. The problem asked: how many units of Product 1 (x₁) and Product 2 (x₂) should a company produce to maximise total profit, while staying within a resource limit and upper production caps?

The objective function to maximise was Z = 7000x₁ + 5000x₂, where each unit of Product 1 earns 7,000 and each unit of Product 2 earns 5,000. Production is constrained by a total resource budget of 360,000 units — Product 1 uses 30,000 units per item and Product 2 uses 40,000 units per item. Additional upper limits cap production at x₁ ≤ 8 and x₂ ≤ 6, and a feasibility condition requires both variables to be non-negative integers (x₁, x₂ ∈ ℤ, ≥ 0).

The model summary confirmed this is an ILP that determines how many units of P1 and P2 to produce to maximise profit while staying within resources — arriving at the optimal solution of producing 3 units of P1 and 6 units of P2 using only non-negative integer values, with a budget of 360,000.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

I learned what makes a problem an Integer Linear Programming model: the variables must be whole numbers, not fractions. This matters because producing 2.5 units of a product is impossible in the real world. The ILP has the same structure as a standard LP — objective function, constraints, feasibility — but adds the integer restriction that changes how it is solved.

Skills

I practised formulating a real production planning problem into a complete mathematical model: identifying the decision variables (x₁, x₂), writing the objective function, translating resource and production limits into inequality constraints, and adding the non-negativity and integer conditions. This structured thinking is directly applicable to real manufacturing and logistics decisions.

Reflection

The most important insight was understanding why the integer restriction matters: the LP relaxation (allowing fractional answers) would give a slightly higher profit, but that answer is meaningless if you cannot produce half a product unit. ILP forces the model to stay grounded in reality. I would apply this framework to any production planning, scheduling, or resource allocation problem where quantities must be discrete.

Branch & Bound — Solving the Pizza Optimizer by Hand 🌿

Hand-traced algorithmic tree · Integer optimization

Branch & BoundInteger ProgrammingUpper BoundPruning

Project Overview

This project applies the Branch and Bound (B&B) algorithm to the same Pizza Optimizer problem from Project 01 — but instead of using a spreadsheet solver, I traced the entire algorithm by hand on paper. Branch and Bound is the method that underlies how computers actually solve integer programming problems like the pizza selection model.

The core idea: because toppings must be selected as whole units (you can't take half a topping), we need a method that guarantees the optimal integer solution — not just a fractional one. B&B works by starting with a relaxed version of the problem (allowing fractional selections), computing an upper bound at each node, then branching on a fractional variable to create sub-problems, and pruning any branch whose upper bound cannot beat the current best integer solution.

This matters because it connects the abstract model structure from Project 01 (binary variables, objective function, constraint) to the real computational method used to solve it. Understanding B&B shows why binary optimization is much harder than simple arithmetic — and why solvers like Excel's Solver are doing sophisticated algorithmic work behind the scenes.

How Upper Bound is Calculated at Each Node

At any node in the tree, the Upper Bound (UB) = current accumulated score + best possible additional score from the remaining toppings.

The "best possible" is found by taking the remaining toppings in order of score-per-ww efficiency, selecting them fully if the budget allows, and adding a fraction of the next topping if the budget only partially covers it.

UB = Score so far + Σ(full remaining toppings) + (remaining budget / next topping cost) × next topping score

If this UB is less than or equal to the current best integer score, the branch is pruned — there is no point exploring it further.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

I learned that the optimal integer solution (score = 26.0) is always less than or equal to the relaxed LP solution (score = 27.25) — because forcing binary choices restricts the feasible region. Branch and Bound guarantees the true optimum by exhaustively but intelligently exploring the tree, pruning any branch whose upper bound cannot beat the best integer solution found so far.

Skills

Tracing the B&B tree by hand required me to calculate the fractional upper bound at every node using the efficiency-ratio ordering of remaining toppings. This developed my ability to evaluate multiple sub-problems simultaneously — a skill that directly translates to how optimization algorithms work inside spreadsheet solvers like Excel Solver.

Reflection

The most surprising thing was realising how much work goes into what looks like a simple yes/no decision. Before this, I assumed the solver just "tried all combinations" — but B&B is smarter: it uses upper bounds to skip entire branches. In a problem with 10 toppings there are 2¹⁰ = 1,024 possible combinations; B&B reaches the answer by exploring only a small fraction of them.
